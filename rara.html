Regional Anesthesia Reference App

Overview

What did you do?

We were tasked by our client – an anesthesiologist from Harborview Medical Center ­– to utilize our expertise to redesign his app.

Why?

Dr. Andrew Walters (our client) felt that his app had usability issues.

What was the overall design process?

We conducted interviews and usability testing to identify key usability issues. We then used the subsequent research findings to inform our redesign.

Who are the users?

Resident anesthesiologists on the rotation for regional anesthesia and anesthesiologists who need a quick refresher on how to perform certain blocks (regions of anesthesia).

What's the user need?

Resident anesthesiologists have to arduously sift through resources filled with deep knowledge and theoretical fluff to be able to find the relevant and practical information they need to perform certain blocks.

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

"Good artists copy; great artists steal" – Pablo Picasso

We took some inspiration from AnSo, which gave users the ability to select and deselect specific parts of ultrasound images.

The Regional Anesthesia Reference App stands out from competitors because it focuses on quick review and has a dosage calculator. Although the app is very niche (not a huge potential user base), it has a unique place in the market.

INTERVIEWS & USABILITY TESTING

Identifying key values and usability issues

Time is a commodity

"What I struggle the most with is to find time to study, read my textbooks, do questions, and consolidate what I have studied."

The value of transparency in healthcare

"I want more transparency of these calculations. I like simplicity but I want to be able to click on things and see how they got that information."

Are you sure it's not a textbook?

"It feels like reading a textbook where you have to scroll through to read the different information as opposed to using an app. I wish you could click overview, or anatomy, and pull up specific things."

"It might be nice to see the same info in a question answer format so I can skim the information better."

Images say a thousand words

"For regional there is a website by the New York School of Regional Anesthesia (NYSORA) that's really helpful and goes through each block with images."

"It’s great because I can look at the app and then compare the ultrasound image with the picture I see on my phone."

"All I care about is what does 'normal' look like, so I usually quickly look through the pictures in an app to see what a normal ultrasound image looks like, or I look at an ultrasound image and compare it with the picture on the app to know what I’m looking at, whether it’s normal."

Anesthesiologists synthesize holistic understanding by comparing images of different views together. NYSORA strategically placed the images in a grid to eliminate scrolling.

WIREFRAMES

Remembering to work within constraints

This a learning moment for me. In my education I've always been taught to work within constraints and to keep implementation in mind, but in this instance, I absentmindedly threw it all out the window. I created these wireframes with aesthetic in mind – tons of gestures and more novel design patterns. I was looking forward to the meeting thinking that my team would love my designs, but it was a needed reality check. My teammates reminded me that our client is an anesthesiologist and a team of one; he created the app himself with limited coding experience. We needed our redesign to be simple and easy-to-implement.

HIGH-FIDELITY PROTOTYPE

Bringing things to life

After finalizing the wireframes as a team and creating the design library with Amanda, the high-fidelity prototype was a matter of combining the two together and then linking the pages in InVision. Creating the necessary instances of each page was time-consuming, but the design library helped speed things along. At this point there were some disagreements among the team about specific details, but we felt that the issues can be brought to light with some more usability testing.

1. Less time hunting. More time doing.

Problem: Chunky text made the app feel like a textbook
Solution: Overview for high-level information, as well as tabs for easy navigation and less scrolling.

2. Revamped calculator experience

Problem: Fat-finger error
Solution: More breathing room of buttons and input fields

Problem: Users were wary of how the dosages were calculated
Solution: A detailed calculations page to increase transparency

Problem: Users were unaware of the recommended max dose settings
Solution: Moved the recommended max dose settings to the calculator page

Problem: Users had to exit the calculator page to refresh
Solution: Added a "clear all" button

3. More freedom to choose how to interact with images

Problem: Users felt that the images weren't interactive enough
Solution: Individual parts can now be selected/de-selected

4. Do more with notes

Problem: Users were unaware of the notes feature
Solution: Design a more robust system for notes and make increase its usefulness

TESTING

Refining the user experience

After another round of usability testing and coming back together as a team, it was clear what we needed to change. One of the main changes we made reverting how the notes feature functions because there was a general sense of confusion about the highlighting interaction and organization of notes from our participants.

If users select a section of text, will they have to press a color for it to highlight or will it highlight with the first color automatically as the default? Can users take notes without highlighting? If so, how would those be organized along with the notes with corresponding highlighted sections? (We felt that they needed to all be in one place.) Due to time constraints, we came to the conclusion that the notes feature was not ready to be implemented; it simply required more research and testing. As designers, we wanted to be able to confidently stand behind the work we were handing off to our client.

1. Scaling back

Problem: Users either opted to not use the notes feature or were confused about how it worked
Solution: Due to time constraints, the notes feature with be reverted back to the original (and simpler) design, but moved to its own tab to increase visibility

2. Rethinking the use of color

Problem: Users thought that each drug having its own color was jarring and overwhelming
Solution: Instead of using color to differentiate various drugs, color will be used to indicate to users the drugs they've interacted with for each "session"

3. Defining secondary interactions

Problem: Users rarely used the show/hide all buttons
Solution: Combine the show/hide all buttons into one toggle to decrease visual clutter and put the primary form of interaction (selecting/de-selecting individual parts) at the forefront

REFLECTION

"We do not learn from experience... we learn from reflecting on experience." – John Dewey

The time constraint of three months to execute our plans put a lot of pressure on how we worked. In addition, our users were very busy people. We had to cut some corners and adapt, which meant not being able to test our wireframes. I think the important thing was that we were able to iterate at least once using the high-fidelity prototype. The debate of testing of wireframes versus high-fidelity prototypes comes with a lot of ifs and buts. Sometimes you aren’t able to do things by the book, which is okay. When you’re unsure, just get something out there and test it.

Next steps would include further testing of the notes feature and to implement a simple, stripped-down version (kind of like MVP, but for a feature). Then of course, we would have to obtain metrics to further identify usability issues. The iterating never stops.

I’ve witnessed firsthand just how busy people working in the healthcare industry are. On-call. Late nights. Facing death on a daily basis. During interviews and usability testing with our participants, I felt how tired and stressed they were. I’m glad that I was able to help them – even if it’s in a small way.

I would also like to thank my team. Shout outs to Amanda, Angela, and Mimi! We made it! I couldn’t have asked for a better team.
