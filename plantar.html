PlantAR

Overview

Role

UX Designer

Vision Statement

PlantAR is the solution that gardeners can use to inspire them to achieve the garden they dream of. Planning where plants should go is an integral part of a gardener's experience – it should be a fun and pleasant. Gardeners can now be sure that when their plants take root, it's the right home for them.

The Problem Space

Gardening is tough. Planning where plants go requires gardeners to invest money into buying plants and time into moving them around, which is labor-intensive. But with the help of augmented reality (AR), gardeners can now bring their imagination to life with a tap of a finger. In addition to utilizing augmented reality, PlantAR also eases the buying process by giving the option to have plants delivered to users' doorsteps.

Challenges
  • Nobody on our team had experience with AR – we spent quite some time to figure out gestures and how to prototype/test
  • Maintaining contact and scheduling repeat meet-ups with the people we interviewed was difficult; we settled on testing with University of Washington students
  • Validating the idea of PlantAR with our target users – a rigid timeline and set deliverables meant we weren't able to conduct user testing
Goals

A lot of what gardeners enjoy about their hobby is being able to connect with nature. PlantAR aims to not completely take over the relationship that gardeners have with their garden, but to make one part of their interaction easier.

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS & INTERVIEWS

Gardeners are doers

Our competitive analysis revealed that there were a lot of tools and resources for gardeners out there, but none of them were ubiquitous or competitive enough. Apps were usually very limited in features and the market was extremely fragmented.

The interviews indicated that gardeners felt that a lot of  solutions take them away from the experience of gardening. For instance, they all preferred to socialize with other gardeners as a way to share knowledge, rather than to use online resources. In addition, we found that every gardener we interviewed were passionate, but faced very specific and individualized problems (animals, weather, plants not growing, etc.).

We knew that our solution would have to integrate into their experience as gardeners in a unobtrusive way, but also help ease the amount of work that gardening comes with.

USER PERSONAS & JOURNEY MAPS

Who exactly do we want to focus on?

It turns out "gardeners" is an extremely broad user group. I interviewed two people who "gardened" as a profession (landscapers), while others were hobbyists. Some gardened because they wanted a beautiful garden, others for food, and some just loved doing it. By creating user personas and journey maps, we were able to really discuss as a team who we really wanted to focus on. After the two user personas were created, we decided to dial in on the needs of users like Lydia.

By contextualizing the bigger picture, the user journey map uncovered that there were interrelated, yet distinct phases in gardening. Using the high-level view, we decided that we wanted to target pain points during the planning phase because it was a hotspot of frustration for Lydia.

STORYBOARDS

Divergent ideation through stories

Storyboards allowed each of us to ideate solutions targeted towards Lydia's pain points during the planning phase, while simultaneously serving as artifacts for us to discuss and critique one another. Three people on my team (including myself) had an idea for a plant delivery service, while one teammate came up with an idea using AR to plan out gardens. Due to the finding from the competitive analysis which indicated heavy fragmentation in the market, we decided to Frankenstein the two ideas together and target several of Lydia's user needs in one solution.

The user needs we decided to target are:

  • Information on specific plants (species and growing conditions)
  • Visualize their planned garden
  • Purchase items without having to worry about transportation
INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

Merging the shopping experience with AR

After honing in on the design requirements, we went more granular to decide the user flows and hierarchy. We were met with the challenge of thinking about how to integrate AR with the shopping experience. Due to the limitations of typing on AR smartglasses, we decided to make it an additional add-on to the user experience – most of the work is done on the mobile app. In addition, by setting the smartphone as the default, we give users the power to choose the fidelity (smartphone camera vs AR smartglasses) of their experience.

PAPER PROTOTYPING & USABILITY TESTING

Quickly weeding out the big issues

Prototyping has always been one of my favorite parts of the UX design process. Since it's the first iteration, we decided to go extremely low-fidelity by making simple paper prototypes of the AR experience. We initially aimed to use transparent film inside the frame to mimic AR, but the transparent film we had was not compatible with the printer we used – it operated at too high of a temperature and melted the plastic transparent film. Scaling back, we had to settle for plain white paper.

After working together to draft up an evaluation plan, we went ahead and conducted the usability tests. Here were the things we learned:

  • Many participants did not have previous experience with AR and needed assistance with interactions
  • Participants found that iconography was familiar and intuitive
  • Some parts of our design did not allow for mass editing or viewing of plants
WIREFRAMES

Defining how to fulfill the design requirements

Wireframes allowed us to completely think through and flush out our ideas. One page that we struggled with in particular was the "add plant" feature that would integrate with the camera. We had to conduct further research into products that were already on the market that had a similar feature in order to draw inspiration for our designs.

REFLECTION

More room to grow

Overall, I'm left feeling a little ambivalent because four interviews is simply not enough to go off of. As a UX designer, I hold research in high regard because it's what informs design decisions; I crave a more robust research phase. The source of my doubt stems from the two people that I interviewed – they were extremely passionate about nature and enjoyed gardening because it was time for them to think, to be in silence, and to connect with nature. However, their feelings and emotions were lost in translation during the affinity diagrams and I can't help but wonder if gardeners as a whole enjoy having technology assist (or intervene, depending on perspective) in their gardening. Is there a fundamental value conflict between gardening and technology? If so, is focusing only on one part of the whole gardening experience with PlantAR doing enough to "not get in the way?"

Can adding a social aspect into the app help alleviate the issue of technology hindering gardeners and their passion? Our findings from the interview indicated that gardeners like to socialize and learn from others. We discussed creating a messaging platform as a way support gardeners in connecting with communities around them, but we felt that Facebook Groups was already fulfilling that user need.
‍
We also had people working in industry critique our final deliverable and I soon realized that our design was not completely flushed out. One thing in particular that I remember clearly was a woman from Amazon stating that our designs did not account for temperature or sunlight – she thought that maybe there would be some way for the AR or smartphone to measure both and indicate to users if a particular spot was optimal.

For my own personal growth, it was an enjoyable learning experience and I'm extremely satisfied with my progress. I imagine that a lot of what I've learned will stay with me because it is so fundamental to the field of UX. I'm ready for the next challenge!
